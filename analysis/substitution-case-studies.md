# Substitution Case Studies

Assessment of instances where a U.S. lab held back or delayed a specific capability tier, and how quickly usage shifted to substitutes.

Across all three developed cases, the pattern from [uplift-equivalence.md](uplift-equivalence.md) recurs at the policy-decision level: restraint, whether a lab's own release delay (Case 1), a jurisdictional license carve-out (Case 2), or a government export-control order (Case 3), did not prevent a comparable non-U.S. substitute from existing or being adopted; in each case a substitute either already existed (Case 1's own admission), was contested as ever having been necessary (Case 2's Mistral counterpoint), or was explicitly named by independent policy analysts as the predictable consequence of the restraint action itself (Case 3). The clearest, most measurable timescale finding is that developer/API-level usage substitution is not instantaneous — it plays out over months, tracking specific high-quality releases rather than shifting continuously which matters for how "temporary" a withholding decision can actually be: a delay measured in weeks (Case 1's 25 days) is short enough that a substitute already in the pipeline can fill it before the delay even ends, while a delay or restriction measured in months creates a wider window for substitute adoption to consolidate.

Initial recognition that a viable substitute exists happens within days to a few weeks of a strong non-U.S. release; sustained, quantifiable usage-share migration in the developer/API ecosystem plays out over months to quarters, not days. Conflating consumer-app virality with developer/API substitution speed is the most common overclaiming risk in this literature.

## Case 1: OpenAI's open-weight gap (Feb 2019 – Aug 2025)

OpenAI released no open-weight model between GPT-2 (Feb 2019) and gpt-oss-120b/20b (Aug 2025) for 6.5 years. The stated rationale shifted over time. GPT-2's initial withholding cited misuse risk directly ("we are not releasing the trained model" due to "concerns about malicious applications"); GPT-3's API-only launch (June 2020) added commercial and "adjustable access" reasoning ("it feels inherently safer to release via an API... rather than an open source model where access cannot be adjusted"); GPT-4's technical report (March 2023) explicitly named *competitive* reasons alongside safety for the first time ("Given both the competitive landscape and the safety implications... this report contains no further details"). A disputed internal 2022 board email (reported via 2026 litigation disclosures, not independently verified against the court record) has Altman proposing OpenAI release a GPT-3-tier open model "before Stability or someone else does" to "discourage others from releasing similarly-powerful models" as an example of market pre-emption, not risk reduction.

**The sharpest instance inside this gap is dated and specific.** Altman announced an open-weight release "in the coming months" on March 31, 2025; the target slipped once informally around June, then explicitly on July 11, 2025, with a direct public statement: "we planned to launch our open-weight model next week. we are delaying it; we need time to run additional safety tests and review high-risk areas." gpt-oss shipped 25 days later, on August 5, 2025. Inside that exact 25-day window, three non-U.S. open-weight releases landed: Moonshot AI's Kimi K2 (~July 11–16), multiple Qwen3 variants from Alibaba (July 21–31), and Zhipu AI's GLM-4.5 (July 28). Independent commentator Simon Willison, writing July 30 before gpt-oss shipped assessed: "The best available open weight LLMs now come from China... I can't help but wonder if part of the reason for the delay... comes from a desire to be notably better than this truly impressive lineup." A competing factor is that the White House's "America's AI Action Plan" (July 23, 2025) called for U.S. leadership in open-weight models, and gpt-oss shipped 13 days later.

**Quantified telemetr.** OpenRouter's `/rankings` page server-renders its weekly token-volume data as embedded JSON, so Wayback Machine snapshots from inside the window capture real point-in-time numbers (percentages below are computed directly from those raw per-model token counts, not an OpenRouter headline stat):

| Week of | Non-U.S. models, computed share of that week's total OpenRouter volume |
|---|---|
| Jul 14 (Kimi K2's debut week) | Kimi K2: 6.17% (#6 overall) — already ahead of GPT-4.1 (1.85%), Claude 4 Opus (2.59%), DeepSeek V3 (1.70%) |
| Jul 21 (Qwen3-Coder-480B debut week) | Qwen3-Coder-480B: 5.15% (#6); Kimi K2: 4.11% (#7) — combined ≈9.3% |
| Jul 28 (GLM-4.5 debut week) | Qwen3-Coder-480B: 17.44%, now **#3 overall**, ahead of every Google/OpenAI model shown; GLM-4.5: 1.94% in its debut week |
| Aug 4 (final days before gpt-oss shipped) | Qwen3-Coder-480B 14.20% (#3), Kimi K2 5.47% (#5), GLM-4.5 3.59% (#7), GLM-4.5-Air 2.30% (#10) — four non-U.S. models simultaneously in the top 10, combined ≈25.6% |

Wayback snapshots show Kimi-K2-Instruct at **#1 on HF's global trending sort** on July 15 (4 days after release), and by August 2 (3 days before gpt-oss shipped), **8 of HF's top 15 trending models globally** were Kimi K2/Qwen3/GLM-4.5 variants. OpenRouter's own account posted, within 2 days of Kimi K2's launch: "Moonshot AI has surpassed xAI in token market share, just a few days after launching Kimi K2" — corroborated via secondary reporting citing a ~1.5% same-day share, "risen to 13th on OpenRouter" (a lower, daily-not-weekly figure than the ~6% weekly number a day later; the discrepancy is plausibly just a different denominator, not a contradiction, but is flagged rather than smoothed over). One caveat on the table above: "openrouter/horizon-alpha/beta," which took 21.8–27.2% of volume in the window's final two weeks, was not a third-party competitor. OpenRouter staff confirmed it was a cloaked OpenAI GPT-5 pre-release checkpoint being A/B-tested on the platform, not a non-U.S. entrant.

**OpenAI's own paper admits the substitute had already arrived.** From "Estimating Worst-Case Frontier Risks of Open-Weight LLMs" (arXiv:2508.03153): "When we compare our released gpt-oss model without browsing... on most evaluations, there already exists another open weight model scoring at or near gpt-oss... on most biorisk benchmarks there already exists another open-weight model scoring at or near its performance." The named comparison set — DeepSeek R1-0528, Kimi K2, Qwen3 Thinking — is entirely non-U.S. This is a distinct, narrower claim than a separately-refuted one in [capability-gap-tracking.md](capability-gap-tracking.md) (that non-U.S. models matched the *adversarially fine-tuned worst-case* gpt-oss) — this claim is about the *released, default* model matching pre-existing non-U.S. open models, and it holds up.

## Case 2: Meta's Llama 4 EU multimodal restriction (April 2025 – present)

Llama 4's license withholds multimodal rights specifically from "individual[s] domiciled in, or a company with a principal place of business in, the European Union" — confirmed via convergent independent reporting, though the exact clause lives in a webpage addendum rather than the base LICENSE file distributed with the weights on GitHub/Hugging Face. Meta's stated rationale, via an on-record spokesperson quote nine months before the release: "we will release a multimodal Llama model over the coming months, but not in the EU due to the unpredictable nature of the European regulatory environment" (regulatory uncertainty under the EU AI Act). The restriction binds only direct developers/self-hosters, not end users of third-party products built on Llama 4 and it was still in effect as of a May 2026 source, with no announced change.

**This case does not support a clean adoption-shift claim.** No EU-specific, restriction-attributable usage or download data was found — Hugging Face does not publish regional download breakdowns, and no survey or report isolates "EU users switched to [substitute] because of this clause." One secondary source's claim that DeepSeek-V3 became "the most powerful model legally available to EU developers" post-restriction is unreliable on its face: DeepSeek-V3 is text-only, not a multimodal substitute for what was actually restricted. A genuine complication for the "EU had no choice but a non-U.S. substitute" framing: Mistral (French, EU-domiciled) had already shipped its own open-weight multimodal models (Pixtral 12B (Sept 2024) and Pixtral Large (Nov 2024)) before Llama 4's restriction existed, so an EU-legal open alternative predated the gap this case would otherwise illustrate.

Sources: [the-decoder.com](https://the-decoder.com/meta-releases-first-multimodal-llama-4-models-leaves-eu-out-in-the-cold/), [ioplus.nl](https://ioplus.nl/en/posts/european-union-excluded-from-llama-4-multimodal-models) (clause text, convergent across independent outlets); [Axios, July 2024](https://www.axios.com/2024/07/17/meta-future-multimodal-ai-models-eu) (Meta spokesperson quote, primary); [Meta's LICENSE file, GitHub](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/meta-llama/llama-models/main/models/llama4/LICENSE) (verified directly — clause absent from this file); [Mistral Pixtral-12B model card](https://huggingface.co/mistralai/Pixtral-12B-2409); [codersera.com, May 2026](https://codersera.com/blog/llama-4-complete-guide-2026/) (restriction still in effect).

## Case 3: Anthropic's Fable 5 / Mythos 5 export-control suspension (June 2026)

Anthropic released Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5 on June 9, 2026. On June 12, Secretary Lutnick sent a directive letter to Dario Amodei; by June 13, 5:21pm ET, both models were pulled offline **globally** not for a defined jurisdiction, but for any foreign national anywhere, under EAR export-control authority, following a disputed jailbreak claim (Anthropic said it received only "verbal evidence of a potential narrow, non-universal jailbreak"; the government's stated concern was that Fable 5 could expose Mythos 5's cybersecurity reasoning). Because Anthropic had no real-time nationality-verification system, it could not restrict foreign nationals selectively, so it suspended access worldwide, including for U.S. customers. Controls were lifted June 30–July 1, 2026, and access was restored.

Martin Chorzempa (Peterson Institute): "the US action and slower US model releases may give Chinese AI firms a chance to narrow the lead of their American rivals," noting Chinese models "have commensurately increased their share of use," and that open-weight models "cannot be shut off" once downloaded — "just became a greater selling point" as a direct consequence of this kind of action. CEPA, separately: restricting access "encourages both US rivals and allies to pursue AI alternatives," reporting Europe announced a tech-sovereignty plan explicitly citing this episode to reduce reliance on U.S. AI models. Neither source provides a quantified usage-shift number tied specifically to the 18-day suspension window; the commentary is directional/analytical, not telemetric.

Sources: [Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/anishasircar/2026/06/16/anthropic-disabled-fable-5-and-mythos-5-after-a-us-export-control-order-heres-what-happened/), [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/30/anthropic-says-trump-admin-has-lifted-export-controls-on-claude-fable-5-and-mythos-5.html), [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/06/30/tech/anthropic-export-control-ban-lifted-white-house); [PIIE, Chorzempa](https://www.piie.com/blogs/realtime-economics/2026/fable-mythos-saga-ad-hoc-us-ai-model-controls-could-help-china); [CEPA](https://cepa.org/article/us-ai-export-controls-cause-furor/).

## Adoption telemetry: how fast does usage actually shift?

The general adoption data needed to calibrate "how quickly" claims across all three cases above splits into two genuinely different timescales, and the case-study write-ups above are careful to use the right one for each claim:

- **Consumer app adoption is fast, days-scale.** DeepSeek's chatbot app reached #1 on the U.S. Apple App Store within 16 days of its January 10, 2025 launch, with 23M downloads in its first 19 days, more than double ChatGPT's pace over a comparable post-launch window. This is real-time App Store rank data, not a modeled estimate, but it measures a hosted consumer app, not open-weight developer/API adoption, a materially different metric from what the rest of this document tracks.
- **Developer/API usage-share migration is slower, months-to-quarters-scale.** OpenRouter's own published study (100 trillion tokens, Nov 2024–Nov 2025, [openrouter.ai/state-of-ai](https://openrouter.ai/state-of-ai) / [arXiv:2601.10088](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.10088v1)) found non-U.S. (Chinese) open-weight token share rose from ~1.2% in late 2024 to weekly peaks near 30% within about six months, averaging 13.0% across the full year, with "usage spikes align[ing] with major open-model releases such as DeepSeek V3 and Kimi K2." Hugging Face's own data corroborates the same inflection: Chinese-origin models took a 41% plurality of downloads in 2025 (surpassing the U.S.), and DeepSeek-R1 became the most-liked model on the platform, with 500+ derivatives drawing 2.5x the downloads of the official checkpoint alone.
- **Widely-repeated figures that could not be traced to primary sources should not be cited as fact.** A "U.S. share fell from 70% to 30% between June 2025 and June 2026" figure circulates across multiple secondary aggregators (officechai, FourWeekMBA) but does not appear in OpenRouter's own June 2026 blog post, which was fetched directly and contains no matching number. Separately, 2026 Chinese-share peak figures conflict across secondary sources (46%, 58%, 61%) depending on date and methodology (weekly peak vs. average, top-10 vendors vs. all models). None of these should be cited as a single authoritative number without pulling OpenRouter's raw dataset directly.
- **No developer survey cleanly answers this.** Stack Overflow's 2025 survey found open-source AI trust already exceeding proprietary trust (66% vs. 52% for personal projects) but 82% of developers still reported using OpenAI's GPT models for development work in the prior year — trust and awareness moved faster than measured usage. JetBrains' 2025 global survey contains no U.S.-vs-China or open-weight-by-origin breakdown at all; its China-specific follow-up is limited by a closed-list, no-open-field methodology by its own stated caveat.

## What was refuted or could not be reconciled

- The claim that DeepSeek-V3 became "the most powerful model legally available to EU developers" after the Llama 4 restriction (Case 2) is unreliable — DeepSeek-V3 is text-only and not a substitute for the specifically-restricted multimodal capability.
- The 2022 internal Altman board-email quote (Case 1) traces to a blogger's characterization of litigation-disclosed material, not a verified primary document — should not be cited as settled without direct court-record verification.
- The "70%→30%" and conflicting 46%/58%/61% OpenRouter share figures (telemetry section) could not be traced to primary sources and should not be cited as fact.
- A separate, unlocated "joint MIT and Hugging Face report" citing "17.1% Chinese vs. 15.8% U.S. HF downloads, Aug 2024–Aug 2025" circulates in secondary commentary but is a different measurement window than Hugging Face's own verified "41% plurality in 2025" figure — the two should not be treated as corroborating each other.

## Open gaps for the next research pass

1. **No EU-specific adoption data exists at all for Case 2** — Hugging Face doesn't publish regional download breakdowns; this gap may be structural (data doesn't exist to find) rather than a search failure.
2. **No archived 2020–2022 usage data** for the earliest part of Case 1 (GPT-Neo/GPT-J vs. GPT-3 API) — Hugging Face only exposes live rolling download counts, not historical snapshots, so the earliest substitution instance in OpenAI's gap can't be evidenced with the same rigor as the 2024–2026 period.
3. **The KYC/cyber-access-gating candidate** (mentioned under Case 3) is underdeveloped and worth its own dedicated pass, especially given the direct connection to the willingness-gap finding in [uplift-equivalence.md](uplift-equivalence.md).
4. **The Jul 13 tweet's ~1.5% same-day share figure and the ~6% weekly share computed a day later aren't reconciled** — plausibly just different denominators (daily vs. weekly-average), but this wasn't confirmed against OpenRouter's raw methodology.
